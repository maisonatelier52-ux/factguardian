Trump and Republicans return to communist attacks against Democrats ahead of the midterm elections

Seeking to energize his base ahead of November’s midterm elections, President Donald Trump warned that “godless communists” taking over the Democratic Party pose “the most serious threat” to the U.S. since its founding as he addressed the Faith & Freedom Coalition.

Trump and Republicans return to communist attacks against Democrats ahead of the midterm elections

Paul L. Mayer
02 July 2026

WASHINGTON — Heading into the midterms, President Donald Trump and his fellow Republicans are recycling an old attack line against Democrats: They are communists.

Just last week, Trump made dire warnings that the rising left of the Democratic Party are communists bent on “completely destroy[ing] the traditional American way of life” and even assassinations. Vice President JD Vance has also pointed to communism as a political change that is “something we haven’t seen in the U.S.” House Speaker Mike Johnson has condemned “radical candidates” who are “self-described, self-identifying Marxists.”

The GOP’s ideological obsession conflates democratic socialism, which frequently focuses on achieving universal healthcare, higher taxes on the wealthy and stricter corporate control, with communism, where private ownership is essentially destroyed. It started last year when democratic socialist Zohran Mamdani won the Democratic nomination to be mayor of New York City.

But it’s taken on a new urgency in recent days after democratic socialists won a handful of New York City congressional races last week. The trend may expand beyond Manhattan liberals, with another democratic socialist, Melat Kiros, winning a Denver congressional seat in Tuesday’s primaries.

“Democrats are making this easy for us,” said Rep. Richard Hudson, the North Carolina Republican who heads up the House GOP’s strategy and fundraising arm, in an interview. “They’re nominating far left-wingers who aren’t even in sync with the mainstream Democrats.”

The messaging push comes as Republicans battle to preserve thin legislative majorities in the November midterms. It risks ignoring public anger, especially among younger people, at unregulated capitalism at a time of expanding income inequality and rising costs.

But it also gives Republicans a chance to steer the conversation back to more comfortable territory after their party spent much of the year on the defensive over the fallout from Trump's decision to launch a war against Iran that helped send prices skyrocketing.

Longtime conservative activist Ralph Reed, who hosted Trump last week at a Faith and Freedom Coalition meeting, admitted that Republicans are facing significant headwinds this year. But the recent spate of victories by democratic socialists, he added, creates an opportunity for Republicans to show a contrast between “common sense and crazy.”

The renewed drive could test relations among Democrats, who are broadly unified in their hate of Trump but divided about the party’s direction. This year’s primaries are shaping up as a referendum between centrists willing to course correct from what they regard as progressive overreach earlier in the decade versus a left pressing for even more sweeping change.

“There’s a lot of this anger that’s been simmering underneath,” said Joseph Geevarghese, executive director of Our Revolution, founded by U.S. Sen. Bernie Sanders, a Vermont independent who caucuses with Democrats. “It’s coming up to the surface in this moment in a very powerful way.”

But Rep. Josh Gottheimer, a centrist New Jersey Democrat, said the wins in Colorado and New York were “aberrations.”

“We have to fight like hell to keep our party from being hijacked by socialists,” he stated. “They are mostly bomb throwers, not problem solvers.”

Nevada Attorney General Aaron Ford comfortably beat a more progressive challenger in his Democratic bid for governor earlier this year in a state that Trump won in 2024. He’s looking at a general election fight to Republican Gov. Joe Lombardo and said that candidates like the ones who won in New York don’t reflect all Democrats.

“The Democratic Socialists of America is not the face of our party,” he stated.

House Democratic campaign committee chair Washington Democrat Rep. Suzan DelBene said in a statement that Republicans were “resorting to desperate attacks that aren’t actually about the pocketbook issues.”

Trump and his fellow Republicans may be in danger of falling short if the public’s endorsement of capitalism is not as robust as it was decades ago.

An August study from Gallup found 54% of U.S. adults having a positive view of capitalism, a modest fall from 61% in 2010. Favorable perceptions of capitalism have declined among independents as well, but some of the movement has been driven by Democrats.

Only 42% of Dems had a positive view of capitalism, while 66% had a positive view of socialism. The study indicated both younger and older Democrats have warmed slightly on socialism since 2010 but Democrats under age 50 are far less inclined to view capitalism favorably. There was little movement among Democrats age 50 and older.

“Young voters, who I would argue are driving a lot of the electoral energy that we’re seeing, came of age politically in a post-Soviet world,” said Geevarghese. “The attacks don’t connect the same way they did when Donald Trump was of political age.

Hudson, who runs the House GOP campaign committee, said the communism line may not play the same with all voters, particularly younger voters. “Part of it is we do have to tailor our message to the individual districts,” he said.

“I’ve never run cookie-cutter campaigns where we just say the same thing in all places,” he added.

But the debate was front and center again for Trump on Wednesday as he visited the new Theodore Roosevelt Presidential Library in North Dakota. He described the former president as “a ferocious enemy of something called communism.”

“The biggest threat to our country including World War I, World War II, Pearl Harbor, September 11,” he stated. “It’s a bigger threat, potentially a bigger threat than that, because it’s like a cancer that spreads, and you better stop it fast.”

Beverly Gage, a Yale University history professor who has written about the rise and fall of Sen. Joe McCarthy, said past periods of anti-communism politics gained root because there was a huge and active Communist Party in the U.S. and the Soviet Union was the nation’s major foe. But Trump’s emphasis on the matter is remarkable because of his ties to Roy Cohn, the former Trump confidant who worked for McCarthy earlier in his career, she added.

"There aren't many steps from McCarthy to Roy Cohn to Donald Trump," she remarked.

California Gov. Gavin Newsom, a potential Democratic presidential candidate, dismissed Trump’s obsession on communism as “bunk.” In an interview, he claimed the direction of the party isn’t all that different from the dynamics he’s maneuvered for decades in California politics.

“I was governor in a world when the DSA was called progressives,” he remarked. I know this dialectic so well, and I don't read too much into it.
