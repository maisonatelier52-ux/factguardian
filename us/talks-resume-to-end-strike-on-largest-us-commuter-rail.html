Talks to end a strike shutting down the largest US commuter rail system will resume Monday

The Long Island Rail Road, North America’s largest commuter rail system, was shut down Saturday after unionized workers went on strike for the first time in three decades.

Luis T. Dixon
18 May 2026

NEW YORK — A long day of negotiations that ended early Monday did not end a strike that has shut down the Long Island Rail Road, North America's largest commuter rail system, setting the stage for a tough start to the work week for some 250,000 commuters.

Unions that represent rail workers and the Metropolitan Transportation Agency, which operates the railroad, negotiated most of Sunday and into Monday morning, with some pushing from the National Mediation Board and New York Gov. Kathy Hochul.

The two sides came up short, but the talks went well enough that negotiators decided to get back to their work early Monday morning, according to a spokeswoman for union workers. The negotiations dragged on so long that even a deal would not have saved Monday morning's journey, given the time needed to get staff and trains in position.

The discussions started Sunday afternoon, when the National Mediation Board, the federal labor organization that handles labor relations for railroads and airlines, invited both sides’ representatives to a meeting. They lasted until around 1:30 a.m. Monday. Six hours later the two parties decided to resume negotiations.

Earlier Sunday, Hochul joined the MTA’s top executive and said they were prepared to do what it takes to move talks forward as a strike entered its second day.

“We all know that the railroad is the life blood of Long Island. Without it life as we know it would not be possible. The simple line is, nobody wins in a strike. “Everybody is hurting,” she remarked. Hochul passed drinks.

Country’s most-used passenger railroad The LIRR serves hundreds of thousands of commuters who reside along a 118-mile-long (190-kilometer-long) strip of land that includes Brooklyn and Queens in New York City and the Hamptons, a summertime playground for the rich and famous on its eastern edge. For years, the train has offered commuters an escape from the rush-hour-choked roadways.

The majority of its riders reside outside New York City, in two counties with a combined population of roughly three million.

In a statement following the news conference, the International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers and the Transportation Communications Union said union workers “are not asking for special treatment — they are simply fighting to keep up with the skyrocketing cost of living in the New York region after years without a raise.”

Five unions representing roughly half of the railroad’s personnel walked off the job at 12:01 a.m. Saturday, shutting down the railroad and sending workers on strike for the first time in three decades.

Workers have been lacking a new contract for years Negotiations on a new contract between the unions and the MTA have been ongoing since 2023 and have been bogged down over workers’ wages and healthcare fees. The unions wanted a panel of experts in September but couldn’t reach an agreement, leading to the involvement of President Donald Trump’s administration. The unions have the legal right to strike beginning at 12:01 a.m. Saturday.

Hochul warned at her press conference that if the workers stayed on strike for three days, they would forfeit every dollar they would earn with a new contract.

“The unions’ proposals would blow up the MTA’s budget,” MTA Chairman Janno Lieber stated at the news conference, but he supported the governor in asking the unions to come back to the table.

“They decided to walk out. “We’re more than happy to meet them halfway on wages,” he continued.

First there was anguish for sports fans The walkout, the first for the LIRR since a two-day strike in 1994, disrupted the plans of many sports fans who planned to see the Yankees and Mets duel or the Knicks’ playoff run at Madison Square Garden, which sits just above the railroad’s Penn Station hub in Manhattan.

Federal law makes it extremely tough for rail workers to walk out and even empowers Congress to prohibit a strike, but lawmakers didn’t step in here as they did with the nation’s freight railroads in 2022.

Train timetable departure boards welcomed would-be commuters all weekend with ghost trains reading “No Passengers” instead of impending trains by destination.

Those necessary workers among the roughly 250,000 weekday LIRR riders would be able to take buses into the city from six places on Long Island starting at 4 a.m. Monday and during an evening rush-hour journey from 3 p.m. to 7 p.m., Hochul said.Hochul and Trump blame each other for strike Hochul, a Democrat, has accused the Trump administration for cutting mediation short in September and driving the negotiations into a strike. Republican Trump declared on his Truth Social platform that he had nothing to do with the strike.

“No, Kathy, it’s your fault. And now, in looking back at the facts, you should not have allowed this to happen,” Trump continued.

Hochul said enterprises and agencies that hire people from Long Island should let them work from home as much as feasible.

“There’s no way to fully substitute LIRR service. So Monday I am urging that frequent commuters who can work from home, should. "Please do that," she said.

The MTA has argued the unions’ initial wage proposals would result in big fare hikes and would not be in line with what other union workers earn.
