House approves war powers resolution to halt military action against Iran in a rebuke of Trump

House approves war powers resolution to halt military action against Iran in a rebuke of Trump

Paul L. Mayer
04 June 2026

WASHINGTON — For the first time Wednesday, the House passed a war powers resolution that would terminate the U.S. military operation against Iran, defying President Donald Trump as a handful of Republicans allied with Democrats to end the three-month-long confrontation that has reordered politics at home and abroad.

House Speaker Mike Johnson had sought to avert a result that would highlight the growing resistance to the war, abruptly cutting off floor action two weeks ago as the resolution was poised for passage. But frustration has mounted in the more protracted battle and Trump’s difficulties in devising a plan for peace.

“It’s time for the president to do what’s right,” he remarked. “The people are sick of suffering because of his war of choice — suffering at the gas pumps, suffering at the supermarkets.”

Wednesday’s roll call was 215-208 but what happens from here is unclear. Trump is unlikely to countenance any congressional move to curtail his commander-in-chief power. But the vote, with four Republicans uniting with Democrats, was a repudiation of the president's war plan and celebrations erupted in the House chamber.

This is the fourth time the House has attempted to limit the U.S. battle with Iran. Last month, the Senate took its own steps toward a war powers resolution, a rare sign of political pushback from the president’s own party, when a number of GOP senators broke with the president.

As Democrats have kept pushing the war powers resolution, more votes have gone against it as political discomfort with the U.S. war has grown. Trump had run for the White House on a pledge to cease U.S. entanglements abroad and focus more on domestic matters, but the war has renewed emphasis on the Middle East.

Johnson said Trump is “laser focused” on the home front, especially with midterm elections approaching that will decide control of Congress.

The speaker said he had a three hour meeting with the president at the White House this week and Trump is calling on friends to help reopen the Strait of Hormuz and resume the flow of business.

Americans have watched petrol prices soar at the pumps since the U.S. joined Israel in initiating the Feb. 28 strikes on Iran, adding to inflationary pressure on consumer spending.

Iran has been able to disrupt shipping via the Strait of Hormuz, a key passage for a major portion of the world’s oil, natural gas and allied products such as fertilizer.

“That last piece is what we’re working on,” said Johnson, R-La. “The strait of Hormuz is open for business and that is in the interest of the entire world. “That's what he's doing.”

A ceasefire in the fighting was declared in April but is fragile and unpredictable. Talks for a more permanent stop to the conflict have lingered on, more hampered by Israel's widening battle with Iran-backed Hezbollah fighters in Lebanon. And as the U.S. and Iran are trading military strikes.

Congress exercises war powers authority The House’s war powers resolution wouldn’t immediately end the conflict, but would be a symbolic – if not legal – measure against additional military action.

The resolution then proceeds to the Senate, where last month four Republican senators joined Democrats to pass a similar bill to curb the U.S. campaign against Iran. The Senate has not yet voted to adopt or reject its own war powers resolution.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio said Wednesday at a House Foreign Affairs Committee hearing that the Iranians would assume that the administration’s “hands are going to be tied” if Congress passed a war powers resolution. They would say ”we won’t be able to do anything to them, so why make a deal?” he remarked.

The campaign against Iran is not the only thing Congress is doing on the national security front, as Democrats in the minority attempt to peel away Republican support for other initiatives.

The House also voted Wednesday on another Democratic-led attempt, a procedural step toward a resolution that would permit U.S. support for the military operations of Ukraine as it faces Russia, and which would help rehabilitate the war-torn country. That vote is scheduled for later this week. Meanwhile, the House was scheduled to consider a war powers resolution to prevent U.S. intervention in Lebanon.

The Constitution gives Congress the right to declare war, but the president, as commander in chief, has the authority to send in the troops, causing a legal clash over which branch of government has the last word on war and peace. If the Senate follows the House and passes the resolution it might lay the stage for a new legal challenge to war powers.

Under the war powers act , the White House has 60 days to get the permission of Congress for military action . However, the administration has said the hostilities have ended since a truce has been announced in the present fight in Iran.
