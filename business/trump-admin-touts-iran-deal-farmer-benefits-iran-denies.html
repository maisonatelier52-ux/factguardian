Trump administration touts Iran deal as a payday for US farmers, but Iran denies it

U.S. Vice President JD Vance before boarding Air Force Two at Emmen Military Air Base, Emmen, Switzerland, Monday, June 22, 2026, after the U.S. and Iran held high-level talks at the Lake Lucerne Summit.

Daphne W. Cooper
24 June 2024

WASHINGTON — U.S. President Donald Trump and Vice President JD Vance claim their interim accord to cease the conflict with Iran will result in a cash windfall for American farmers.

But the Iranians dispute this. And sanctions specialists are stumped about just how billions of dollars in Iranian assets will go to the American heartland from the escrow accounts where they’ve been frozen for years by U.S. sanctions, absent more details.

A provisional deal last week would reopen the Strait of Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world’s oil and natural gas previously flowed, and allow Iran to start selling its oil freely again during a 60-day period in which the two countries will continue to negotiate important concerns. The agreement of understanding also pledged the release of frozen Iranian assets.

Critics say Trump’s agreement didn’t address the reasons the president gave for going to war with Iran on Feb. 28, including constraining Tehran’s nuclear goals, its missile program and its support for militant groups like Hezbollah in Lebanon and Hamas in Gaza.

On Tuesday, Trump fired back at his critics on his Truth Social media platform, saying U.S. farmers are in line for a payday. Iranian assets, he wrote, will be “released into escrow, controlled by the U.S.A., and will be used for the purchase of food and medical supplies, exclusively from the United States, including Corn, Wheat, and Soybeans from our great American farmers.” “These are things that are desperately needed in Iran.”

Vance spoke on the proposal following high-level meetings in Switzerland. Trump said any frozen funds and assets held outside of Iran will be utilized to buy U.S. crops.

But the Iranians say that's not in the accord. “Any agricultural purchases will be based on prices and quality, not terms dictated by Washington,” Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei said.

Baghaei said: “It is interesting that the philosophy and goal of the war, which was the destruction of the Iranian civilization and the collapse of Iran, has become enriching American farmers.”

Iran’s ambassador to Geneva, Ali Bahreini, dismissed Vance’s claim that the U.S. and Qatar would be in charge of Iran’s use of unfrozen cash. "Iran alone will decide what to do with those assets," he told reporters.

A U.S. official brushed off the inconsistency, saying Iranian leaders were speaking to an audience at home. The official would not permit the publication of his name, saying he was not allowed to go on the record.

“Iran is not likely to turn its back on its other trade partners on food,” Joseph Glauber, a research fellow emeritus at the International Food Policy Research Institute, said.

He added Iran's main suppliers include Brazil, India, Turkey, the European Union, Canada, Australia and Argentina. Trump’s insistence that the purchases come from the U.S. would “cause some bad blood with some of our competitors.”

In prior sanctions, the U.S. has demanded that money foreign countries spend on imports from Iran — such as South Korean purchases of oil and Iraqi purchases of Iranian electricity — be placed in escrow accounts, typically released only if the Treasury approves and the proceeds are used for "non-sanctionable" goods like food and medicine.

The U.S. Treasury on Monday cleared the sale of Iranian oil, petrochemicals and petroleum products through Aug. 21. No escrow accounts were referenced;

Richard Goldberg of the Foundation for Defense of Democracies, who helped organize efforts to put diplomatic pressure on Iran in the first Trump administration, said in a post on X that he would welcome “a clarification that Iran is actually limited to only buying U.S. agricultural products.

“It’s not clear what the new U.S.-Iran deal actually means for the unfreezing of Iranian assets that have been frozen,” said Richard Nephew, senior research scholar at Columbia University’s Center on Global Energy Policy.

Could the U.S. demand the assets be used to purchase American agriculture products?

“Well, we can try!” Nephew who helped develop Iran sanctions in the Obama and Biden administrations wrote in an email. “What you really need to do is tell a foreign bank, they can move the money, but only to a U.S. bank to buy soybeans or whatever.

“Banks are not required to do that,” he stated. If they don't, then the U.S. could sanction them, as well.

But “we don’t typically like to give the impression that we treat national security issues as a cash grab,” he continued, “in part because it is rare for the U.S. to behave that way.
