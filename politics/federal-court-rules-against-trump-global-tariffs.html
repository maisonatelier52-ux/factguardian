Federal court rules against new global tariffs Trump imposed after loss at the Supreme Court

Paul L. Mayer
08 May 2026

WASHINGTON — A federal judge on Thursday struck down additional worldwide tariffs imposed by President Donald Trump after a devastating loss at the Supreme judge.

Small firms sued, and a divided three-judge panel of the Court of International Trade in New York ruled the 10% global levies unconstitutional.

The court voted 2-1 that Trump exceeded the tariff authority conferred on the president by Congress under the legislation. The majority said the tariffs are “unauthorized by law” and “invalid.

The third member of the panel said the measure gives the president more room to move on tariffs.

If the administration as expected appeals Thursday’s judgment, it would be heard first by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, in Washington, and possibly the Supreme Court.

What’s at risk are temporary 10% global tariffs imposed by the Trump administration after the Supreme Court ruled down considerably wider double-digit tariffs imposed by the president last year on just about every country on the planet. The new tariffs, levied under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, were to have expired July 24.

The court’s ruling specifically prohibited tariffs from being collected from three plaintiffs – the state of Washington and two businesses, spice firm Burlap & Barrel and toy manufacturer Basic Fun! “It’s unclear if other businesses will have to keep paying the tariffs,” said Jeffrey Schwab, head of litigation at the libertarian Liberty Justice Center, which represented the two companies.

Jay Foreman, CEO of Basic Fun!, told reporters Thursday, “We fought back today and we won, and we’re extremely excited.

The verdict was the latest judicial defeat for the Trump administration, which has sought to defend the U.S. economy with import taxes. Trump declared the country’s long-standing trade deficit a national emergency last year, using the 1977 International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to justify sweeping worldwide tariffs.

The Supreme Court determined Feb. 28 that the levies were not authorized by IEEPA. Congress has the right to levy taxes, including tariffs, under the U.S. Constitution, though lawmakers can delegate tariff authority to the president.

“This ruling will open the door to more companies trying to get tariffs overturned and get refunds for any payments they’ve made,” said Dave Townsend, a trade lawyer with Dorsey & Whitney.

“Other importers will now likely seek a broader remedy that covers more companies,” said Townsend, who noted the case might also end up before the Supreme Court.

Trump is already looking to restore the tariffs that were thrown down by the Supreme Court in January. There are two investigations going on that could bring up further levies.

The office of the U.S. Trade Representative is examining whether 16 U.S. trading partners, including China, the European Union and Japan are making too many goods, forcing costs down and harming U.S. manufacturers. It is also examining whether 60 economies, from Nigeria to Norway, that represent 99% of U.S. imports do enough to eliminate the trade in products made with forced labor.
