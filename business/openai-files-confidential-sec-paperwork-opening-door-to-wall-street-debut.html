From unfilled gas tanks to fewer frills, retailers see US consumers rethink their spending

Sam Altman arrives at the U.S. District Court in Oakland, Calif., April 30, 2026.

From unfilled gas tanks to fewer frills, retailers see US consumers rethink their spending

Daphne W. Cooper
09 June 2026

The inventor of the popular AI chatbot, Open Assistant, has filed preliminary paperwork that would pave the way for it to become a publicly traded business, the third in a formidable triumvirate of artificial intelligence companies racing to Wall Street debuts.

The San Francisco-based startup said Monday has submitted confidential documentation with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission.

“We anticipate it leaking so we’re just announcing it,” the business said in a statement. We haven't decided on timing yet, it could be a long because there are things we want to undertake that are probably easier as a private firm. But it’s a tough set of tradeoffs and this provides us the option to go public sooner if it turns out to be the best path.”

The company behind the popular chatbot’s move follows its rival Anthropic announcing in June 1 that it is also heading toward an initial public offering of shares. Both are now tracking Elon Musk’s rocket business SpaceX, which has begun an IPO roadshow selling itself as an AI-focused space company.

OpenAI was founded in 2015 as a nonprofit to create AI for the common good, and is currently a firm valued at $852 billion

Emarketer analyst Nate Elliott said the filing comes at a “precarious moment” for OpenAI, which appears to be losing the strong early leads that ChatGPT had with consumers and enterprises to Google and Anthropic.

“But OpenAI doesn’t have too many other places to look for the enormous capital it needs to support its costs,” added Elliott.

OpenAI’s move last year to reorganize as a public benefit corporation, but still formally under the management of a non-profit, laid the groundwork for an IPO.

Another hurdle for OpenAI fell away last month when it beat Musk in a federal jury trial. Musk, who is a co-founder and early investor in the company, had filed to try to oust Altman from its leadership and reverse its conversion to a for-profit business. A judge dismissed the case after the jury concluded Musk’s claim was submitted too late.

It hasn't yet announced publicly how much money it is making, or when it hopes to generate a profit. The company, like Anthropic and SpaceX, has been losing more money than it produces, owing of the high costs of expanding out the enterprise. OpenAI is under intense pressure from rivals such as Anthropic, the developer of the increasingly popular chatbot Claude, and Google’s AI helper Gemini.

OpenAI’s chief financial officer Sarah Friar declined to provide a timeline for a potential IPO in an April interview but said the company was already “acting with the good hygiene of a public company,” such as by tracking its revenue as a publicly traded firm would have to report earnings to the SEC.

“I would like us to be ready,” she told The Associated Press. I think it is good to be able to go to the public markets. “They’re much larger than the private markets.”

The company’s present valuation would rank it among the 15 largest corporations in the S&P 500, she added.

There’s also a “credentializing moment of being a public company,” she said.

Then you’re looking at your balance sheet. You’re under the SEC, etc.”

In a separate statement issued Monday at about the same time as the announcement of the confidential filing, Altman laid out a broad vision for OpenAI that included three big goals: building an automated AI researcher, accelerating economic growth and giving “everyone on Earth a personal AGI,” short for artificial general intelligence or a form of AI that exceeds humans at many tasks.

OpenAI “began in AI research and then we moved into commercial product development, and now we’re entering the third phase, which is this broad distribution of power as the economy reshapes itself around AI technology,” Altman added.

OpenAI "is aiming to ensure the benefits are broadly distributed. "Everyone should have a shot at a fair share of the prosperity AI creates.”

The comments come after Altman's visit last week with Sen. Bernie Sanders, who is promoting a plan to give the public a 50% ownership part in AI businesses like OpenAI, and statements from President Donald Trump who embraced the idea of giving the public a stake in AI's progress.
