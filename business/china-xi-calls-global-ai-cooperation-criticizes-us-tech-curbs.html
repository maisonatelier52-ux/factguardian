China’s Xi calls for more global efforts to guide AI, chides US for its curbs on tech sharing

Chinese President Xi Jinping called for global cooperation on artificial intelligence, warning against countries placing their own national security interests above those of others.

China’s Xi calls for more global efforts to guide AI, chides US for its curbs on tech sharing

Daphne W. Cooper
18 July 2026

SHANGHAI — Chinese President Xi Jinping on Friday urged for the development and administration of artificial intelligence to be a global effort, underlining China’s objections to what he termed the “overstretching” of national security concerns.

In a speech in Shanghai, Xi said AI should not be dominated by any one country. Restrictions spearheaded by the US have cut China off from some of the world's most advanced technologies, driving China to develop its own know-how and heightening the rivalry between the world's two largest economies.

“Developing artificial intelligence should not be a solo performance by one country, but a symphony of global cooperation,” Xi said in a message, delivered at the opening of China’s biennial World Artificial Intelligence Conference in Shanghai. Also attending were the leaders of Kazakhstan, Cambodia and Thailand, and U.N. Secretary-General Antonio Guterres.

He echoed a long-standing Chinese complaint that “we should jointly oppose the practice of overstretching the concept of national security when artificial intelligence, and of prioritizing one’s own security over that of other countries.”

Xi said China would boost AI collaboration with the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, the League of Arab States, the African Union, the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States, the Shanghai collaboration Organization and the BRICS countries. He granted 30 countries access to a Chinese-developed AI weather technology that provides early warning systems.

China will offer 5,000 training opportunities for developing countries in artificial intelligence over the next five years, Xi said.

He claimed closer ties can assist to avoid “historical injustice in AI.”

China’s new AI group seen as response to U.S. China signed an agreement with 29 countries including Pakistan, Russia and Kazakhstan ahead of the summit to set up a World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization. It was promoted by state media as an intergovernmental organization located in Shanghai to advance global governance on AI.

“It could be viewed as China’s response to the U.S.-led Pax Silica initiative,” said George Chen, partner and chair of digital practice at The Asia Group, a consultancy headquartered in Washington.

The Pax Silica framework, announced late this year, is intended to boost cooperation with U.S. allies and partners on supply chains connected to artificial intelligence. Japan, the U.K., Australia, the Philippines, Israel and India are among the signatories.

China and the United States have agreed to hold a discourse on AI development and governance following a visit to Beijing by U.S. President Donald Trump to meet Xi in mid-May.

Chen, who was speaking at the conference in Shanghai, also said Xi’s address might be interpreted as a signal that China can be a trusted partner to the developing world or “Global South” countries. “China will not allow artificial intelligence (AI) technology to be monopolized by the US.”

China doubles down on self-reliance, flaunting high-tech prowess More than 1,100 firms and 1,400 guests are attending this year’s biennial AI conference, Chinese state media stated.

The tech giant Huawei is presenting its powerful AI computing system, the Atlas 950 SuperPoD, at the conference which continues till Monday.

Some tech observers now say China is an innovator in AI, not simply trying to catch up to the U.S. China’s five-year plan until 2030 has made advancement in frontiers of science and technology, including AI, a priority.

Open-source AI models like DeepSeek from China are viewed as attractive and often cheaper alternatives to the predominantly closed-source AI models from the U.S., particularly in the developing world.

Chinese AI startup Moonshot launched its latest AI model, Kimi K3, in parallel with the conference. The 2.8 trillion parameters of Kimi K3 – one of the measures of an AI model’s capabilities – will make it the world’s largest open-source model, it added. DeepSeek’s V4 Pro version contains 1.6 trillion parameters.

Another Chinese AI company, Zhipu, or Z.ai, this month launched its new flagship GLM-5.2 open-source model, challenging U.S. rivals including Anthropic’s models.

But U.S. officials and even large U.S. AI companies like Anthropic have accused Chinese AI models of criminal "distillation" of their models to extract their technologies, an accusation Beijing calls "groundless." U.S. politicians have also highlighted worries about the economic danger posed by Chinese AI to the United States.
