US sanctions Cuban President Miguel Díaz-Canel in latest move to pressure island’s leadership

Paul L. Mayer
05 June 2026

WASHINGTON — The United States imposed sanctions Thursday on Cuban President Miguel Díaz-Canel, his wife and three other individuals in the latest action by the Trump administration to pressure the island’s government that brought quick denunciation from Havana.

Among those sanctioned is Alejandro Castro Espín, the only son of former Cuban President Raúl Castro and Vilma Espín. He was an adviser to Cuba’s Defense and National Security Commission and was present when Raúl Castro greeted then-U.S. President Barack Obama in Havana on a historic visit in March 2016. Also mentioned was Raúl Alejandro Castro Calis, son of Castro Espín.

The additional sanctions come as U.S. President Donald Trump has been threatening military action in Cuba after removing Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro in January and then establishing an energy blockade that choked off petroleum supply to the island. This has caused island-wide blackouts, food shortages and economic collapse.

The threats carried added weight after the U.S. last month announced criminal charges against Raúl Castro. Trump signed an executive order to broaden sanctions against the island. The penalties announced Thursday freeze individuals’ property and bank accounts in the U.S. But how integrated their finances are with the U.S. banking system is uncertain.

“It’s pretty unlikely” that Cuba’s president and others have assets in the U.S., said Richard Feinberg, former U.S. national security adviser on Latin America and professor emeritus of international political economy at the University of California, San Diego.

They might be interpreted as precursor to an intervention or putting pressure on the government to reach a deal,” he said. The rhetoric of Trump and U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio “could take you in either direction,” he said.

Díaz-Canel charged that Trump was making “new threatening statements against Cuba” and claimed “these measures are aimed at reinforcing the blockade and escalating the conflict between Cuba and the United States.”

He wrote on X that “to this political blindness are added the coercive measures implemented in recent weeks against our country, aimed at hurting the Cuban people”. “The U.S. government’s aggression and perversion will meet our determination to face the worst-case scenarios and resist the imperial onslaught.”

Trump on Cuba: ‘We’re going to handle that’ Trump was asked Thursday if his sanctions were intended to hasten the collapse of Cuba. “We just want them to be a nice country, a well-run country,” he answered.

“The country is starving and has no energy, no oil, no money, nothing. It's got a nice piece of real estate. "You could have beautiful resorts," Trump told reporters at another event in the Oval Office.

Asked if Cuba was near to collapse, he said: "It's sort of collapsed. "We're going to tackle that as soon as we've concluded" military operations in Iran, he continued.

“I like to do one thing at a time,” Trump stated.

Trump has ramped up rhetoric about regime change in Cuba after threatening to orchestrate a “friendly takeover” of the island if its authorities don’t open the economy to American investment and get rid of U.S. opponents.

Rubio, the son of Cuban immigrants, has long been a hawk on Cuba’s socialist leadership, and has stated Trump’s preference is to strike a deal but has expressed skepticism that the U.S. can reach a diplomatic resolution with the current regime.

Those “designated today direct or finance the regime and its efforts to mobilize its radical revolutionary movements in the United States and around the world,” Rubio said in a statement.

Rubio has backed the Trump administration’s decision to impose a series of escalating sanctions on Havana, the largest of which targets Grupo de Administración Empresarial S.A., a commercial conglomerate controlled by the Cuban Revolutionary Armed Forces.

Cuba’s top diplomat blasts new sanctions as creating a conflict scenario Diaz-Canel was not the only one. “The vile inclusion” of him and others — including Cuban institutions and civil society organizations — “is the latest example of the U.S. interventionist plan to portray Cuba as a threat to the U.S. national security,” said Bruno Rodríguez, Cuba’s minister of foreign affairs.

“Every attempt by the US to provoke a conflict between the two countries will fail,” he said on X. “Our people will be even more united and determined against any threat to Cuba’s independence and sovereignty.”

The new sanctions also freeze any assets individuals targeted may have in U.S. jurisdictions or that may come into U.S. jurisdictions, and apply to non-U.S. businesses that might conduct business with them.

The sanctions hit not only the individuals but also Cuba’s defense ministry, its Institute for the Friendship with the Peoples, which promotes people-to-people talks; Amistur Cuba, an arm of the institute that manages specialized tourism on the island; and the Committees for the Defense of the Revolution.

Cuba’s president’s list includes Castro relatives, others Díaz-Canel was selected in 2018 to replace Raúl Castro and was the first person in decades to rule Cuba without the Castro surname.

Under him the island entered the worst economic and energy crisis in modern history, made worse by increased sanctions from the Trump administration.

Also on the list of punishments was Díaz-Canel’s wife, Lis Cuesta Peraza. She is not named first lady, because title was abolished during the revolution, but she performs the function in actuality, receiving other spouses like Queen Letizia of Spain and accompanying her husband on important excursions.

Miguel Anido Cuesta, son of Díaz-Canel's wife and his stepson, also subject to sanctions.

“It’s almost an honor to be on this ‘list,’ Cuesta Peraza wrote on X late Thursday. They never get tired of derision and political stupidity.”

The new step puts more pressure on the Cuban regime but isn’t the first time the U.S. has placed sanctions on leaders of state or government and their kin.

The U.S. sanctioned former Sudanese President Omar Bashir and former Zimbabwean President Robert Mugabe in the early 2000s and more recently sanctioned Maduro and his wife.
