The US lost a surprising 92,000 jobs last month as the unemployment rate ticked up to 4.4%

Daphne W. Cooper
21 min ago

WASHINGTON —Last month, 92,000 positions were abruptly terminated by American firms, indicating that the labor market is still struggling. The rate of unemployment increased to 4.4%.

The Labor Department said Friday that hiring had declined from January, when businesses, NGOs, and government organizations added a healthy 126,000 positions. In February, 60,000 additional jobs were anticipated by economists.

69,000 positions were also eliminated from the December and January payrolls due to revisions.

The unexpectedly poor February employment report exacerbates the economic uncertainties brought on by the war with Iran, which has driven up oil prices and hit consumers and businesses with unanticipated expenses.

According to Heather Long, chief economist at Navy Federal Credit Union, "the job market is struggling in the face of so many headwinds." "Until the war is over and businesses can see that consumers are still spending, they will be even less willing to hire this spring." The U.S. economy is going through a difficult period.

After being battered by President Donald Trump's unpredictable trade policies, his purge of government employees, and the lingering impacts of high interest rates in 2025, the employment market was predicted to improve this year. Employers only added 15,000 jobs each month in 2025. After January hiring exceeded projections, hopes for a 2026 recovery increased.

Olu Sonola, head of U.S. economics at Fitch Ratings, said, "This report delivers a knock-down blow to that view just when it looked like the labor market was stabilizing." "It's bad news whichever way you look at it."

There were many job losses.

Last month, construction companies laid off 11,000 workers, probably as a result of the cold weather. Additionally, following a four-week walkout by over 30,000 nurses and other front-line employees at Kaiser Permanente in California and Hawaii, healthcare companies laid off 28,000 employees. One of the job market's advantages has been the health care sector.

For the past 14 of the last 15 months, factories have eliminated 12,000 positions. Nearly 30,000 jobs were lost in pubs and restaurants. approximately 19,000 jobs were lost by administrative and support service companies, while approximately 17,000 jobs were lost by courier and messenger services.

Although employment losses are still occurring in the financial sector this year, financial firms gained 10,000 new employees.

The average hourly wage increased 3.8% from a year ago and 0.4% from January.

The war with Iran has a negative impact on the employment market and the economy as a whole.

The Federal Reserve faces a conundrum as a result of the war's rising inflationary pressures and poor hiring. It must choose whether to lower interest rates to boost the labor market or wait to help control prices. According to Raymond James head economist Eugenio Aleman, "this is probably the worst scenario for monetary policy."

Because of the uncertainty around Trump's tariffs and the erratic manner in which he implemented them, employers were hesitant to hire last year.

In 2026, the effects of Trump's tough trade policies might lessen. After he struck a trade agreement with China last year and reached agreements with major U.S. trading partners including Japan and the European Union, his import duties decreased and became less unpredictable. Many companies have also discovered ways to offset the tariffs' costs, frequently by raising prices for customers.

Trump's 2025 tariffs, according to Boston College economist Brian Bethune, were a shock to businesses' business planning. Now that they've adapted to them, "You know what! Suddenly, a rise in fuel prices brought on by the conflict with Iran upends their plans for 2026.

The CEO of Basic Fun, a toy maker, Jay Foreman, anticipates some respite from Trump's tariffs after the Supreme Court overturned the largest ones last month and may pave the way for importers to receive reimbursements for the taxes they paid. Foreman's Boca Raton, Florida-based business, which produces Lincoln Logs and Care Bears, will be able to invest more thanks to the reimbursements. He can also hire new staff members and give workers larger raises.

He declared, "We anticipate a record year."

However, Foreman predicts that Basic Fun's tariff bill will more than double this year to $15 million under the new tariffs that Trump is pursuing. This is partially due to the fact that the company will be footing the bill for a full year of Trump tariffs in 2026. Last year, tariffs weren't implemented until the spring or later.
