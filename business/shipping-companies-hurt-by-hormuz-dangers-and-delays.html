Shipping companies are being hurt by fluctuating positions and dangers while they wait for Hormuz to open again

With hundreds of vessels still stuck in the Persian Gulf and costs piling up, shipping companies are being whipsawed by uncertainty over how and when the Strait of Hormuz might reopen more than two months into the Iran war.

Shipping companies are being hurt by fluctuating positions and dangers while they wait for Hormuz to open again

Daphne W. Cooper
10 min ago

NEW YORK — Shipping businesses are being hit hard by uncertainty about how and when the Strait of Hormuz might reopen more than two months into the Iran war. Hundreds of ships are still stranded in the Persian Gulf, and prices are rising.

President Donald Trump unveiled "Project Freedom" on Sunday. This is a technique for the U.S. to "guide" ships out of the strait. Two ships made the trip, but Trump suddenly stopped the effort on Tuesday to give time for a settlement to end the conflict.

In the meantime, the dangers for ships and their crews are still there. The French shipping company CMA CGM Group said on Wednesday that one of its cargo container ships was damaged when it was attacked while trying to cross the strait. Major ship owners and operators are saying that the strait is still too dangerous because of concerns about Iranian speedboats and drones.

Sean Pribyl, a maritime lawyer with Holland & Knight in Washington, D.C., said, "In the end, it's still going to come back to the main issues of risk and safety" that shippers have to think about.He continued, "It looks like we're not even close to being able to navigate and drive freely through the strait again."

Costs pile up as goods, oil and ship workers remain stranded

Lloyd's List Intelligence, a research company, says that before the war in Iran, 100 to 135 ships passed through the Strait of Hormuz every day. But that number has dropped to almost nothing since Iran required ships to go through a vetting process run by the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps in order to get safe passage. Ships must follow a path close to Iran's shore, give information about their crew and cargo, and in some cases, pay a price. At the same time, giving money to the IRGC could get you in trouble with the U.S. and the EU, which have labeled it a terrorist group.

Oil and oil products, like fertilizer, are stuck in the strait, along with thousands of ship personnel. On Tuesday, Air Force Gen. Dan Caine, who is the head of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, indicated that there are more than 1,550 ships in the Persian Gulf with roughly 22,500 sailors on them.

The U.S. Navy is blocking Iran's ports to put pressure on the country. The blockade is being enforced outside the strait in the Gulf of Oman and the Arabian Sea.

Pribyl from Holland & Knight noted that shippers and ship insurers are probably still looking at the situation in the strait. Protection and indemnity insurance covers property and third-party liabilities, whereas war risk insurance covers damage and losses caused by war.

Due to the risk of attack, insurance costs for ships in the area have gone up from less than 1% of the value of goods on board to between 3% and 10% during the conflict, according to Ed Anderson, a professor of supply chain and operations management at the University of Texas's McCombs School of Business. But most shippers still think the crossing is too dangerous, even with insurance.

He said, "Taking a couple of ships out on the ferry hasn't really changed the shipping industry at all."

A long return to normal

Kaho Yu, head of energy and resources at risk intelligence company Verisk Maplecroft, said that oil prices and shipping are unlikely to go back to normal until it is evident that the threat of strikes in the Strait of Hormuz has lessened.

He remarked, "Even with diplomatic efforts still going on, energy markets are unlikely to quickly return to their pre-crisis assumptions." "Refiners, shippers, and commodity traders will stay cautious until there is more clear proof that Hormuz disruptions won't happen again."

Iranian and Chinese diplomats met on Wednesday to talk about how to calm things down. Yu went on to say, "Hormuz is the real metric that will be watched." "Over the next few weeks and months, tanker traffic and energy flows will probably be more important than diplomatic language in figuring out if Beijing can turn its influence with Tehran into real stability."

Razat Gaurav, CEO of Kinaxis, a company that manages supply chains, said that shipping won't "snap back overnight" if the ceasefire persists and ships start moving through the Strait of Hormuz again.

He said, "Even when things get better, carriers, insurers, and shippers need to be sure that stability will last until capacity and routes are back to normal." "Air cargo can get back on track pretty quickly, but ocean shipping usually takes weeks or months because of longer lead times and contract restrictions."

He noted that shipments of some types of goods, such liquid natural gas and sulfur, which come mostly from the Middle East, are likely to move faster as backlogs clear. However, "most shippers will remain cautious until stability proves durable," he said.
