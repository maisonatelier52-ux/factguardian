Former Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan dies at 100

Economist Alan Greenspan, chairman of the Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, is seen in his office in Washington, Friday, Oct. 18, 2013.

Former Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan dies at 100, leaving behind a lasting legacy in global economic policy.

Daphne W. Cooper
21 min ago

WASHINGTON — U.S. Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan dies at 100.

His wife of 29 years, NBC News correspondent Andrea Mitchell, reported he died Monday of symptoms from Parkinson’s Disease.

Mitchell added, "He was my husband to me, the one that impacted my life from the first date in 1984. He exhibited 'irrational excitement' for baseball, the Washington Commanders, tennis, golf and music, particularly jazz. He will be remembered for his intelligence and generosity. “Being his life partner was my joy in life.”

Greenspan’s 18½ years leading the Fed covered a long period of American progress and wealth, but it was a period that ended in catastrophic fashion in 2008, two years after he departed the central bank.

So revered was Greenspan during his lengthy tenure as head of the world's most powerful central bank, that by the time he retired in 2006 he was universally praised as the "Oracle" and "Maestro".

But Greenspan’s reputation took a big hit when the U.S. housing bubble burst, sparking a worldwide financial crisis that almost destroyed the U.S. banking system and pushed the country into the worst recession since the 1930s. Critics blamed much of the disaster on Greenspan’s easy-money policies and what they saw as an overly confident belief in poorly regulated financial systems.

Later Greenspan himself conceded that he was “wrong” to believe the nation’s banks, the foundation of the financial system and the entire economy, could effectively manage themselves.

During his 18½ years at the Fed, Greenspan oversaw a spectacular run-up in stock values and a 10-year economic expansion that began in March 1991. He was hailed as the “Maestro’’ and the “Oracle,’’ a maestro who tended to America’s economic well-being, and whose almost every statement was scrutinized for signals as to where interest rates, the economy and the financial markets were headed.

Greenspan’s objectives were speculated so hard that it spawned new Fed folklore: The “Briefcase Indicator.” Greenspan brought charts and data to bolster his point, and a filled briefcase taken into Fed meetings made it clear that change might be in the offing.

Greenspan’s reputation suffered a blow almost immediately upon his departure from the Fed in 2006. US home values began to decline, then fell into a dizzying plunge that inflicted large losses on banks, pension funds and other investors who had gambled heavily on real estate. Housing values fell and millions of Americans lost homes to foreclosure, many of them weighed down by outsize mortgage debt. The rising financial crisis sent the U.S. economy into the Great Recession of 2007-2009, the worst downturn since the Great Depression of the 1930’s.

The U.S. problem quickly spilled over abroad, igniting a debt crisis for nations in Europe, and prompting Beijing to create a large government stimulus package to steady its economy.

In retrospect, opponents faulted much of the crisis on Greenspan’s easy-money policies, his faith in the virtue of lightly monitored financial markets and his inattention to the irresponsible risk-taking that had been thriving in the financial system under his watch. Greenspan later acknowledged that ``I made a mistake'' in thinking the nation's banks, whose stability underpins the financial system and the overall economy, could very much manage themselves.

Until then, however, it appeared that Greenspan could do no wrong. In the United States and around the world, he was viewed with a combination of wonder and reverence. Many even feared openly the day he would depart the Fed.

Investors heard his often impenetrable observations. The most remembered such comment was when Greenspan, in effect, sent financial markets plunging on Dec. 5, 1996, when he implied, with two words — “irrational exuberance” — that stock prices were too high.

Greenspan was careful to use obfuscation, knowing his ability to sway markets. Sometimes he even satirized himself for doing so. “I know you think you understand what you think I said, but I don’t know if you realize what you heard is not what I meant,” Greenspan once told a confused congressional committee.

Greenspan was born in the Washington Heights district of Manhattan and was a math prodigy, his mother would bring him out to dazzle visitors.

“I was a prop at parties,” he said PBS NewsHour in a 2007 interview. He was a teenaged professional musician, playing clarinet and saxophone with the future jazz great Stan Getz, a humbling experience that led the young Greenspan to seek another line of work, and a Julliard School dropout.

He studied as an undergraduate at New York University, where he earned a doctorate in economics as a graduate student. For most of three decades he maintained an economic consultancy firm. In the 1950s he became a disciple of the libertarian philosopher Ayn Rand, who dubbed him the "Undertaker'' for his dark attire and subdued manner. Rand was by Greenspan’s side when he was sworn in as President Gerald Ford’s main economic adviser in 1974.

In 1987, President Ronald Reagan tapped Greenspan to run the Fed. He was soon put to the test. The stock market had the greatest one-day percentage loss in American history in the first two months of his administration on October 19, 1987, called “Black Monday.” The Dow Jones Industrial Average quickly fell 22.6% from its peak, for reasons that were not totally evident then, and are not yet.

Greenspan earned praise for bringing back calm and stability. He reassured Wall Street that the Fed would provide as much money to the financial system as needed to restore calm. The American economy was unaffected by the market crisis and stocks recovered.

Greenspan’s crisis management talents were challenged again in 1997 and 1998 with a financial crisis in Asia threatening to spread economic destruction all around the globe. In the early days of the crisis, Greenspan negotiated an emergency loan for Thailand and convinced U.S. institutions to roll over short-term loans to a wobbling South Korea.

As a Fed governor, Greenspan was praised for presiding over what was then the longest economic expansion in U.S. history, a 10-year period of prosperity from March 1991 to March 2001. During that stretch the nation’s jobless rate momentarily dipped below 4 percent for the first time since 1970.

And inflation, which had bedeviled the United States and most of the world economy in the 1970’s, was astonishingly quiescent under Mr. Greenspan’s tenure, something many economists did not think could continue for so long.

Greenspan contended during the extended boom that technological advances had resulted in such efficiency gains that the economy could operate at higher speeds, with reduced unemployment, without sparking inflation. The idea was that the Fed could maintain interest rates low even when the economy was flourishing.

As Fed chair, Greenspan loved to plow through arcane economic data, from monthly boxcar loadings to steel output, to tell where the economy was headed. He would frequently call economists from various agencies of the government to go over the details. He got up early each morning for a two-hour soak in the bathtub, reviewing statistics and Fed staff memoranda.

Greenspan also improbably made the gossip pages as something of an unexpected ladies' man. He dated TV journalist Barbara Walters and, following a 12-year relationship, married NBC News’ Andrea Mitchell. They didn't have kids.

Greenspan dated Walters while he was an adviser to President Gerald Ford. "I don't believe it," Ford wrote on a newspaper clipping about the couple which he clipped out and handed to his chief of staff, Dick Cheney, according to "The Man Who Knew" a biography of Greenspan by Sebastian Mallaby.

Throughout, Greenspan clung to the idea that financial markets were almost self-regulating. He helped stymie attempts by Brooksley Born, the nation’s top commodities regulator, to extend federal oversight to the underground market in over-the-counter derivatives in the late 1990s — with officials from President Bill Clinton’s White House. Derivatives enabled speculators to bet on anything from the price of oil to high-risk mortgages.

Eventually history would vindicate Born, not the Maestro.

The low interest rates Greenspan has generated a dangerous bubble in the pricing of homes. The deregulation of finance that he championed permitted banks and other financial companies to take on massive risks, frequently beyond the eyes of government regulators. Derivatives bets sank insurance behemoth American International Group, which needed a $180 billion federal rescue.

“Congress created the Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission to investigate the debacle, and the commission concluded:

“More than 30 years of deregulation and reliance on self-regulation by financial institutions, promoted by former Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan and others... had removed key safeguards that could have helped prevent catastrophe.

In the years after he retired as Fed chairman in 2006, just shy of his 80th birthday, Greenspan stayed busy doing what he loved to do most – tracking the economic data. He operated a consulting firm, Greenspan Associates, giving advise to clients on Wall Street and collecting lucrative speaking fees.

He was busy into his 90s, penning his memoir and two other works on the economy and giving his views on the current economic happenings on television news broadcasts.

He also signed comments and opinion pieces supporting the Federal Reserve’s political independence amid persistent attacks by President Donald Trump. He signed a statement in January 2026 denouncing the Trump administration’s probe into Fed Chair Jerome Powell. The statement, signed also by two other former Fed chairs and five former Treasury secretaries, branded the inquiry “an unprecedented effort to use prosecutorial attacks to undermine” the Fed’s independence and cautioned it would have “highly negative consequences for inflation.”

Greenspan’s Fed chairmanship - August 1987 to January 2006 – was only five months short of the longest Fed chairman. That title went Governor William McChesney Martin, who served from 1951 until early 1970.

In his 2013 book “The Map and the Territory,” Greenspan defended himself against critics who blamed him heavily for the 2008 financial catastrophe. He suggested that the old-fashioned ways of economic prediction simply could not compete with the irrational risk-taking that can lead to disastrous price bubbles.

“Bubbles go up very slowly as euphoria builds,” Greenspan told the Associated Press in an interview in 2013. Then the fear comes, and it comes down quite sharply. When I started to look at that I was kind of intellectually shaken.”
