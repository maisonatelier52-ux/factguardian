Trump says US military strike killed leader of Tren de Aragua gang with help from Venezuela

Paul L. Mayer
13 June 2026

WASHINGTON — President Donald Trump announced Friday that a “swift and lethal kinetic” U.S. strike killed “the infamous leader” of the Tren de Aragua gang, Héctor Rusthenford Guerrero Flores.

The United States has designated Tren de Aragua as a terrorist group. Guerrero Flores faces charges of racketeering conspiracy and other offenses, including offering support to terrorists in acts that spanned more than a decade, prosecutors said in December in a New York federal court.

The strike on a Tren de Aragua complex in Venezuela took place earlier this week, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth said in a post on X.

The group is behind “countless acts of violence, extortion and drug trafficking in North America, South America and Europe,” U.S. Attorney Jay Clayton stated at the time. Trump on Thursday nominated Clayton to be director of national intelligence.

The U.S. State Department had offered prizes of up to $5 million for information leading to Guerrero Flores' arrest.

"Tren de Aragua terrorists are no longer safe haven in Venezuela or anywhere else, and under my leadership we will find these vicious murderers and drug lords anytime, anyplace and send them to the depths of hell where they belong," Trump said in a post on his social media site. Guerrero Flores was identified by his alias, “Niño Guerrero,” in Trump’s post.

The article also featured unclassified video recorded from above of a little structure with a green roof being blown apart.

“This operation is a testament to the commitment of the United States and Venezuela to take the fight to narco-terrorists and deny them safe haven in our hemisphere,” Hegseth added.

The Venezuelan government issued a statement confirming its involvement in the operation, saying it took occurred in the southeastern state of Bolivar.

During the operation, there were skirmishes with members of criminal groups, which resulted in the death of Héctor Rusthenford Guerrero Flores, alias ‘Niño Guerrero,’ leader of one of these criminal organizations,” the statement said.

The mineral-rich state borders Brazil and Guyana and is home to enormous illicit mining operations that have long been run by gangs and other actors that dig with the consent — and to the benefit — of government and the military.

Trump has taken a series of unusual moves against the gang, including a series of strikes on tiny boats his administration has accused of bringing drugs into America. Since the Trump administration began targeting what it calls “narcoterrorists” in early September, the U.S. military has killed at least 207 people in boat attacks in the eastern Pacific Ocean and Caribbean Sea.

Trump and government officials have repeatedly blamed Tren de Aragua for the violence and illegal drug dealing that has plagued some U.S. cities. The president has spent months repeating the charge — denied by a declassified U.S. intelligence assessment — that Tren de Aragua has operated on the orders of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro. Maduro was taken out of Venezuela in January by the U.S. to face U.S. narcotics allegations.

Tren de Aragua was born more than a decade ago in an infamous chaotic prison with hardcore criminals in Venezuela’s central state of Aragua. The gang has grown in recent years as millions of Venezuelans flee to neighboring Latin American countries or the U.S. seeking better living conditions.

Guerrero Flores was sent to the prison in Aragua for murder and other crimes in 2013 when Venezuela’s crisis began as corruption, incompetence and a decline in crude prices devastated the oil-dependent economy. The government ignored the prisons and Guerrero Flores and a few other prisoners spotted a business opportunity.

They took over and ran the jail . They established a system where the entire convict population was controlled by force and extortion . In the years, they turned the place into a kind of metropolis with a zoo, baseball field, casino and restaurants. Guerrero Flores had his own sumptuous apartment.

The size of the group is unknown. Countries with large concentrations of Venezuelan migrants, particularly Peru and Colombia, have blamed the group for a spate of violence in the region. But unlike other criminal organizations from Colombia, Central America and Brazil, Tren de Aragua has no large-scale participation in smuggling cocaine across international borders, according to InSight Crime, a think tank that studies crime across Latin America.

Gang leaders in Venezuela have a reputation for unlawful activity, long known to be involved in everything from gold mining to drug trafficking.

Legal mining of gold and other minerals is part of a staged plan by the Trump administration to turn around the crisis-wrecked country. In March, U.S. Secretary of the Interior Doug Burgum, on a visit to Venezuela, said the government of acting President Delcy Rodríguez was offering security assurances to mining corporations interested in investing in the South American country.

Trump campaigned for re-election on a promise to get tough on immigration and crime. His favorability ratings have dipped on his handling of the economy according to polls, but immigration remains Trump's greatest subject, the AP-NORC Center for Public Affairs Research said.
