Nvidia’s Jensen Huang says society needs ‘new social norms’ in the age of AI

In an exclusive interview with The Associated Press, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang says the AI age demands new social norms and encourages people to embrace the technology’s potential instead of fearing it, insisting it will create exciting “new opportunities.”

Nvidia’s Jensen Huang says society needs ‘new social norms’ in the age of AI

Daphne W. Cooper
17 June 2026

SHERMAN, Texas — Jensen Huang, the CEO of Nvidia, whose work helped power the rise of artificial intelligence, said in an Associated Press interview Tuesday that society needs to change with the rise of AI, claiming that a greater embrace of the technology would make people’s lives better.

Huang has been bullish about AI's capability to quickly reshape society, spurring quicker economic growth and greater scientific achievements. But as the leader of a computer chip company currently building AI systems, he and others are facing a public that is growing more worried about the potential harm the technology could cause. Huang has had to reply to critics who warn of the loss of jobs and the threat to mankind itself.

“We have to create new social norms,” Huang said in an interview. "AI, I would tell everyone to use. “Just go and involve it.”

Huang made his case as AI has become a political flashpoint with objections to plans to build more data centers and that the speed with which it’s being adopted could lead to layoffs of workers who might not have a safety net. These questions have undermined public support for the technology at a time when a race has begun with China, a contest that Mr. Huang thinks can best be won by an America that is open to global competition in AI.

His tight ties to President Donald Trump have also drawn criticism from Democrats, even as he insisted that the computing power generated by AI is key to adding the factory jobs that have been promised for decades without any lasting success. The point was made by a 63-year-old guy who has witnessed the evolution of the technology and classified himself as “boring” as his life is largely centred on job and his family.

In the conversation Huang opened up a bit about himself, including what his favorite movie is, “Kingdom of Heaven,” the 2005 epic about the 12th century Crusader Kingdom of Jerusalem. He stated he had seen the movie “Project Hail Mary” three or four times and “I think we might watch it again this weekend.”

Huang stated that AI’s abilities to design a website, evaluate complex documents, guide sophisticated research, or even plan a kitchen remodel had helped to bridge the technology divide in America. Now, he added, people may do advanced work on computers without knowing how to program or develop software.

Huang said some government oversight and safety standards were needed for AI, and that national security also needed to be a priority for the technology that has been propelling stock market gains and U.S. economic growth in recent years.

Just like it adapted to the vehicle, Huang argued, society will adapt to AI. Cars used to be shown killing children, he added, but the world modified its conventions by putting in sidewalks and crosswalks and keeping youngsters from playing in the streets.

Huang questions what government control of AI businesses might do Nvidia’s market cap is almost $5 trillion and it has surged in value over the past several years to become the world’s most valuable company. OpenAI and Anthropic, two businesses that build AI models, could also potentially cross $1 trillion with their equities trading publicly.

The growth in wealth at companies working on AI has triggered new concerns about inequality in the economy. Trump has sought to assuage those fears, lately speculating about the US government owning some shares in AI companies so that any windfalls would be more widely shared with the public. Sen. Bernie Sanders, I-Vt., and even OpenAI CEO Sam Altman have also floated that concept.

Huang was skeptical of the concept, saying he believed the country will already benefit substantially from AI advances.

"I'm not quite sure what they're trying to do with government ownership," he remarked. “I haven’t had a conversation with them about that. But remember, these are American companies. A lot of Americans are investors in that stock price and benefit from its performance. It creates taxes which supports a lot of Americans. “It’s generating a lot of jobs.”

AI companies might also result in better earnings for energy, building and hardware technology enterprises, he said.

“Americans already have a stake in American companies naturally in a whole bunch of different ways,” Huang added.

Huang thinks AI should be a national security priority The Trump administration has recently switched from a light touch on AI regulation to a stronger hand.

It slapped export limitations on the latest models of AI company Anthropic, prompting the business to shut off all public access to those models Friday over security concerns. Republican Trump also signed an order requiring the government to voluntarily test new AI models before they are release

“The government is rightly focused on national security issues but it is important to give clear guidance,” Huang said.

“All technologies should always have national security as the number one concern,” added Huang. “But that said, you know, you need to be very specific about what risk you are concerned about before you put in place policies for export controls.”

Nvidia under the Biden administration fought export regulations meant to stop it from selling chips to China, dismissing the administration’s claim that a restriction would protect an American edge on AI. Huang had cautioned that the export limitations could impede America’s capacity to build the world’s AI ecosystem, as China would counter with its own powerful chips.

“The key problem for America’s AI development is energy,” Huang argues. Huang said the United States is susceptible due of its poor energy supply. That's a lot of electricity to ask from the power grid. The data centers doing the computations utilized in AI.

Some data centers will be built with their own sources of electricity, but Huang said the U.S. is at a disadvantage on energy to start with. And with less energy, it’s difficult to play to U.S. strengths in its AI infrastructure, models and computer chip development.

“The U.S. is woefully behind in energy production,” Huang stated. “We just suffocated energy production for too long.

Huang also praised Trump’s plan to produce more energy in the U.S.. The president has been a big fan of using oil, coal and natural gas, but has mocked the use of solar and wind power.

The Nvidia chief executive was not commenting on Trump’s hostility to environment friendly types of energy. But the disparity he identified speaks to some of the anxieties U.S. residents have about AI boosting their utility bills.

Huang spoke Tuesday during a visit to an extension of the Coherent factory in Sherman, Texas, to build a laser to carry data between chips, which could reduce power consumption of AI systems by as much as 50%.

Trump’s liking for Huang started at a meal in Mar-a-Lago Trump, not known for his technological prowess, rapidly built a friendship with Huang. The president has hailed him "smart" and "amazing" and has insisted that Huang join him on international travels. Most recently, Trump had Air Force One pick up the leather-jacketed CEO in Alaska on his way to his state visit to China.

Their romance began last year with an invitation to dinner at Mar-a-Lago, Trump’s home and private club in Florida. Huang was in the area to accept the Edison Achievement Award for his work on AI.

“He told me to come over for dinner. I did,” Huang recalled. He traveled with Lori, his wife.

“He was very engaging, very charismatic, conversational, asked a lot of questions,” Huang said. “From the moment that I met him, all he’s ever talked to me about is creating more jobs, reindustrializing the United States, protecting national security, winning.” “He calls me in the middle of the night and wants to talk about one of these topics,” he said.

But his connections to Trump have also raised eyebrows among Democratic leaders. Sen. Elizabeth Warren, D-Mass., criticized Huang for not coming before a Senate committee when “he has time to attend to a $1 million-a-head dinner at Mar-a-Lago.”

“I want the U.S. president and other officials, regardless of party, to succeed,” Huang stated. “We may disagree with politics, but we should want him to succeed,” he remarked. “Because when President Trump wins, our country wins.”
