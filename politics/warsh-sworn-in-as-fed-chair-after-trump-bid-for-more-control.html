Warsh is sworn in as the Fed chair after Trump’s bid for greater control over the independent bank

President Donald Trump celebrated the White House swearing-in of the new Federal Reserve chair Friday, saying he wants Kevin Warsh’s help stimulating the economy while insisting his hand-picked chair and the central bank will remain independent.

Warsh is sworn in as the Fed chair after Trump’s bid for greater control over the independent bank

Paul L. Mayer
23 May 2026

WASHINGTON — President Donald Trump on Friday presided over a White House swearing-in of the new Federal Reserve chair and said he would want Kevin Warsh's help in reviving the economy even as he tried to stress that the nation's central bank would remain autonomous.

Trump spent months blasting Warsh’s predecessor, Jerome Powell, for being hesitant to slash interests rates, with the Republican president contending that lower borrowing costs would bring an economic boost. Trump went to the uncommon step of hosting the ceremony in the East Room instead of at the Fed, making apparent his joy that Warsh is in charge.

The confrontation with Iran has drove petrol prices higher, rattled financial markets and fueled inflation fears throughout the economy. Those factors have fueled recent worries about whether Warsh will answer Trump’s pleas and push the Fed to cut rates.

But Trump said he thought Warsh would favor a robust economy.

“Thankfully, unlike some of his predecessors, Kevin knows when the economy is booming, that’s a good thing,” the president remarked. "You don't have to go crazy," Trump said. Just let it be. We want this to explode.”

Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas administered the oath of office Also attending were House Speaker Mike Johnson, R-La., Justice Brett Kavanaugh, CIA Director John Ratcliffe and Cabinet members.

“I think he’ll go down as one of the really great chairmen of the Federal Reserve we’ve ever had,” Trump said of Warsh.

In 1987 Alan Greenspan was sworn in as Fed chair at the White House by Republican President Ronald Reagan. The 2006 event at central bank headquarters when Ben Bernanke took over as chair was attended by Republican President George W. Bush.

But the White House event raises further issues about the Fed’s independence at a time when Trump has continually pushed to bend the independent central bank to his will.

The Justice Department under Trump opened an inquiry into Powell and the Fed’s massive facility renovations. That sparked backlash from lawmakers and the department dropped the investigation. The Fed’s internal watchdog is investigating. Powell’s time as chair ended last week but he has chosen to remain on the Fed board for now.

Trump took pains to clarify during his comments, “I really mean this, honestly. I want Kevin to be totally independent, this is not said in another way.

“I’d like him to be independent and just do a great job,” Trump remarked. 'Don't look at me, don't look at nobody. Just do your own thang.”

But in the next breath, Trump said that “in the eyes of many, the Fed has lost its way in recent years” under his predecessor, Democratic President Joe Biden. Trump also claimed that Warsh wants to lead policies that foster “positive economic growth” and that doing so didn’t have to involve higher inflation.

Trump also pointed out that the stock market was up Friday. “That’s their way of saying they like you,” he added of Warsh.

At the time, Warsh sharply criticized Fed policies, including its low interest rate policies coming out of the coronavirus pandemic, which Warsh says helped fuel the largest U.S. inflation spike in four decades in 2021-2022. More recently he has echoed Trump’s calls for lower rates on occasion.

Warsh says the gains in productivity from artificial intelligence will help the economy grow faster without causing inflation, letting the Fed cut borrowing costs. But many Fed officials disagree that AI’s development will help rate cuts, especially as the technology has also been blamed for widespread layoffs in the computer sector and elsewhere in the economy.

On Friday, Warsh offered “to lead a Federal Reserve committed to reform, learning from the past’s successes and failures, avoiding static frameworks and models and maintaining clear standards of integrity and performance.”

“These are years that I think can bring unparalleled prosperity that will lift the living standards of Americans across the spectrum,” he told Trump. And the Fed has something to do with that.”

Warsh also noted that the Fed’s mandate “is to promote price stability and maximum employment. “When we pursue those aims with wisdom and clarity, independence and resolve, inflation can be lower; growth stronger; real take home pay higher and America can be more prosperous.”

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, who was leaving the ceremony, echoed Trump’s message, telling reporters Warsh will “do the right thing for inflation and growth.”
